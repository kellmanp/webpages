Why real time?  With the varying techniques currently available for cardiac imaging , what is our motivation for real time imaging?   We want  a non ECG triggered, free breathing method of imaging, that will avoid triggering problems in pts with arrhythmia.  We also want  a rapid acquisition to avoid  breathing artifacts in heart failure and  pediatric patients who can’t hold their breath.
Segmented SSFP is the traditional method of acquisition for cine imaging in cardiac MRI and works very well for patients with regular heartbeat rythm. Segmented scans are breath held, ECG gated, over multiple heart beats.   With each heartbeat we obtain a set number of phase encode lines, based on the  views-per-segment.  To keep it simple, this example show  4 PE lines acquired each heartbeat.   During the first heartbeat, we obtain the first  4 lines of K-space at each phase of the cardiac cycle.  During the second heartbeat we obtain the  4 next lines, until we have filled the matrix. The resultant cine images provide high quality with good spatial and temporal resolution.
 
Patients with arrhythmias present a challenge in obtaining segmented images.    In most cases images are degraded by artifacts caused by inconsistent cardiac shape, size and motion during abnormal heart beats.   The lower diagram demonstrates segmented imaging in a patient with arrhythmia,  which causes image blurring artifacts.
This slide illustrates real time imaging with and without acceleration. In real-time imaging, the complete set of phase encode for each image is acquired in a free-running manner which does require ECG triggering or breath-holding.  For fully sampled K-space (shown at the top)  the spatial and/or temporal resolution may not be adequate, thus accelerated imaging may be used by under-sampling the phase encodes to go faster as shown in lower diagram.  The boxes represents the time of each image.   Under-sampling causes wrap artifacts which may be unwrapped using the SENSE reconstruction method. This example used 4-to-1 acceleration to go 4x faster.
These are the typical parameters we use for real time imaging. 3 different matrix sizes are available for use, resulting in different temporal resolution and corresponding frame rates.  We typically use the 192 X80 matrix with approximately 50 ms temporal resolution or 20 frames per second. While this represents a compromise from what may be achieved with segmented cine, it has the benefit of not requiring ECG or breath-holding.
Our images were obtained on Siemens Avanto 1.5T magnet with 32 channels.
The performance of the SENSE method of parallel imaging relies on the number and geometry of the coils. We used custom surface coil arrays to improve performance in order to work at 4-to-1 accelerations. We used both 8 and 32 channel custom arrays. The 32 element coil, with 4 X4 anterior and posterior arrays, from Invivo Corp is shown here.  We also use an 8 element cardiac array from Nova Medical Systems, which could be used on either the Sonata or Avanto. I’ll show examples from each.
This is  a real time true-FISP image.  It is non-ECG  triggered, and as you can see it is free breathing. This is acquired using the 8 channel Nova Medical array using rate 4 TSENSE acceleration.
Another benefit of real-time imaging is the ability to acquire many slices quickly by eliminating the pauses between breath-holds. We obtained this entire stack of short axis images in a single free-breathing 20 second acquisition. In this case we chose to acquire a couple of cardiac cycles per slice. These images were obtained with a 160 X 90 matrix using the 32 channel coil.
This is another example of real-time rapid acquisition for a pediatric patient. This 13 year old with Friedrichs ataxia was unable to breath hold.  Using real time, we obtained this stack of SAX images in under 30 seconds.
This slide demonstrates the benefit of real-time imaging for patients with arrhythmia. On the left is a patient with PVCs. Real-time imaging is not affected by the irregular rhythm.  The irregular heartbeats are clearly seen in the images which correspond with the recorded ECG.
The patient on the right had a bigeminy arrhythmia pattern. Again you can see the abnormal rhythm in images and corresponding ECG.
While the conventional ECG-gated and segmented cine may be used with arrhythmia rejection, the arrhythmia rejection is not always reliable and may greatly lengthen the breath-hold. The real-time approach mitigates this problem.
In  conclusion, despite its limitations of compromised spatial and temporal resolution, and the need for optimized coils, real-time imaging has benefits over conventional  segmented cine-imaging.  It has proven clinically useful in  patients with arrhythmias and is also useful for cases such as pediatric or heart failure, or any other condition where breath holding is a problem. It is a simple, rapid method of acquisition, capable of  obtaining an entire stack of images in tens of seconds.
