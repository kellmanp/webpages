•This talk is about a method of accelerated imaging which I refer to as TSENSE based on combining SENSE with temporal filtering, such as UNFOLD.
•There are a number of possible ways to combine these methods.
•For instance, they can be combined to achieve either:
•higher acceleration, or
•they can be combined to achieve additional ghost artifact suppression.
•I will focus on how combined spatio-temporal filtering can be used to achieve increased artifact suppression, which makes the implementation both:
•Robust, and
•Adaptive
•I will begin by discussing rate 2 acceleration, and then conclude by briefly describing the extension to higher acceleration.
•This method is applicable to:
- real-time imaging
    - such as realtime free breathing exercise stress function to be described, or
- Retrospective cine
•This simplified diagram illustrates the TSENSE method for a rate 2 acceleration.
•The k-space is acquired with multiple coils using an interleaved phase encode order, odd-even-odd-even, etc.
•The reduced FOV images may then be reconstructed with SENSE
•Any residual artifacts due to errors in coil sensitivity estimates will be temporal frequency shifted to the band-edge, and can be further suppressed using a temporal lowpass filter. The order of temporal filter and SENSE may of course be reversed.
•The coil sensitivity maps may be estimated adaptively as shown using the surface coil images alone (without acquiring separate body coil reference acquisitions), by using a temporal lowpass filter.
•The narrower lowpass filter bandwidth can be used to produce highly ghost suppressed images with lower temporal resolution, which may be used to compute the SENSE coefficients. I’ll say more about this in a few moments.
•Example thumbnail images are shown to illustrate the method. Better quality images will be shown in a moment, along with quantitative measurements of artifact suppression.
•This example is for  non-breath-held, continuous,  real time imaging of a short axis slice of the heart, immediately following exercise stress, so the heart is beating at 150 bpm and the respiratory rate is 30 breaths/min, with heavy chest breathing. This was conducted on a GE scanner using their standard cardiac 4-surface coil array.
•You can observe the beating between the image and ghost in the magnitude images, caused by the alternating phase of the alias ghost.
•After lowpass filtering the ghosts are suppressed.
•The adaptive SENSE images achieve the full temporal resolution, and have reasonable quality, but there is still some residual alias artifact since the chest wall is moving so rapidly, and there are errors in measuring the relative coil sensitivities. You can see that the residual artifact is flickering, since it is shifted to the bandedge.
•This residual artifact is further suppressed using a temporal lowpass filter with 80% bandwidth.
•Next let me discuss the adaptive computation of SENSE coefficients which is central to this method.
•In order to estimate sensitivity maps using surface coils only, it necessary to remove the image magnitude and phase components.
•This is necessary for 2 reasons:
1) Any dynamic component in the image will cause the desired image to be temporal modulated, and even filtered.
2) It is often desirable to apply some degree of spatial smoothing.
•I use the following scheme:
•The sample correlation matrix is computed at each pixel which removes the image phase, but preserves the coil phase differences.
•At this point spatial smoothing can be applied
•The sensitivities are then computed as the dominant eigenvector.
•And finally used to compute SENSE coeffs incorporating noise statistics.
•The phase map illustrates how much variation in phase there is in the heart over a few cardiac cycles. The final estimate of relative coil phase has the temporal modulation removed.
•These short axis images were acquired approximately 15 seconds after exercise stress. •They are acquired continuously at 31 frames per second in real-time during heavy breathing using a FGRE sequence with an ETL=8. The spatial resolution was 128 x 96  with 1/2 phase FOV acquiring 48 ky-lines, 24 even and 24 odd, and so on. •The alias images are shown for reference to illustrate how intense the surface coil intensity is at the chest wall, which requires a high degree of suppression. •The adaptive SENSE and UNFOLD methods applies individually achieve a high degree of artifact suppression, nevertheless there is residual artifact at particular phases. •The adaptive TSENSE with combined spatio-temporal filtering is able to achieve additional artifact suppression.
•The artifact suppression may be analyzed quantitatively by examining the temporal spectrum. •This spectrum corresponds to the myocardium region with most severe chest wall ghost. The desired myocardium component in the center, which is modulated at the heart rate, is approximately 10 dB weaker than the intense chest wall component which is temporally shifted to the bandedge, in this case +/- 15.6 Hz. •Note that the chest wall component is actually modulated at the heart rate as well, due to the heart thumping on the chest and possible blood flow. This modulation, while weak, is not readily filtered by temporal filtering, and is the source of artifacts when using temporal filtering such as UNFOLD by itself.
•The magnitude response of the temporal lowpass filter used for artifact suppression is overlaid for reference.
•The temporal filter provides excellent suppression at the bandedge
•However, heart rate modulation components fall within the passband, and residual artifacts are evident in the images.
•The adaptive SENSE provides artifact suppression that is more independent of temporal frequency •However, with we are achieving only about 30 dB suppression with the adaptive method, which is not sufficient at the bandedge, leaving residual artifacts that were observed in the images.
•The TSENSE method with combined spatio-temporal filtering achieves excellent suppression across the full temporal spectrum. •This combination makes for a robust and adaptive scheme which tolerates errors.
•The temporal spectrum shown here corresponds to a region in the periphery of the FOV. •In this case the alias component is the heart which has a wide temporal bandwidth and cannot be suppressed with temporal filtering alone. •This illustrates the well known bandwidth sharing principle which is the basis of UNFOLD. •However, in the case of TSENSE, the SENSE method provides sufficient suppression •Unlike using a strict UNFOLD implementation, our TSENSE method uses a wide temporal bandwidth across the full- FOV.
•Let me speak briefly about extension to higher rates of acceleration
•The are numerous combinations of phase encode order and spatio-temporal filtering. Let me give a few examples which achieve an overall R-4 acceleration.
•For R=4, every 4-th phase encode is acquired.
•If we alternate every 2 frames with an offset of 2-lines, the odd ghosts at 1/4 FOV  and 3/4 FOV are temporally shifted to the bandedge, while the FOV/2 does not experience any phase shift and remains at “d.c.”. In this case SENSE may be used to cancel the 1/2 FOV ghost and temporal filtering such as UNFOLD may be used to cancel the odd ghosts. I presented experimental results for this combination last year. This scheme is not adaptive since it cannot be used to derive a ghost free reference and has a very constrained dynamic FOV. •An alternate approach is to cycle every 4-frames stepping a single line each frame, thus acquiring all k-space lines every 4-frames. This scheme may clearly be used for adpative TSENSE. Each ghosts is temporally shifted to a different frequency as depicted. The 1/2 FOV  ghost will be at the bandedge, while the odd ghosts at 1/4 FOV  and 3/4 FOV will be at quarterband. •While one could perform R=4 SENSE and R=4 UNFOLD, using a comb notch, the use of notch filters is not particularly desirable, and furthermore, the outer 3/4 FOV would still be constrained to be relatively static. •Another approach is to temporally filter only the bandedge removing the 1/2 FOV ghosts. Either a R=3 or 4 SENSE may then be used. With R=3, the outer 1/2 FOV  must be relatively static, but the loss in SNR due to variance inflation (so called G-factor) will be improved.
•The benefits of the TSENSE method are:
•increased robustness since increased alias artifact rejection can be traded for:
•errors in sensitivity profile estimates due to a variety of artifact mechanisms
•a less stringent temporal filter
•it is fully adaptive
•which is useful for heavy breathing
•or interactive manipulation of scan plane
•and is simple to use, eliminating the need for separate acquisition of body coil references.
