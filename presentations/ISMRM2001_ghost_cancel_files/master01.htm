•I will present a method for ghost artifact cancellation based on phased array combining, which is similar to the method known as SENSE used for accelerated imaging. •I will show results that demonstrate the use and benefits of this method in a cardiac imaging application.
•Let me begin by describing how the basic ghost cancellation method works. •In phased array or multi-coil reception, the desired image as well as ghosts are weighted by the coil sensitivity for individual coils as depicted for this example with 4 coils and FOV/2 ghosts. •The individual coils sensitivity weights both the desired image as well as ghosts, denoted here by si(x,y). •This weighted superposition is a set of linear equations which can be solved (in a least square sense) by a matrix inversion if the sensitivity profiles are known. •The desired image and ghost are also weighted by their respective values of complex point spread function, h0 and h1 in this case. Therefore, the equations actually solve for  the PSF weighted desired and ghost images, h0 f  and h1 f. •Unlike the SENSE accelerated imaging application for which the ghosts are caused by intentional undersampling with a space invariant PSF, this method is quite general and may be used with a spatially varying PSF. That is to say h0 and h1 are functions of (x,y).
•This block diagram illustrates the phased array ghost cancellation method for a FOV/2 ghost example. •This example uses an oil/water phantom which is acquired using multi-shot EPI with a short echotrain length of 2, and a non-interleaved phase encode order to illustrate the cancellation of space variant ghosts. I will discuss more about phase encode strategies in a few moments. •The strong oil ghost is due to the chemical shift, while the much weaker water ghost is due to the slight off-resonance due to shimming errors. •On the top are the multi-coil images with ghosts and on the bottom are the same images shifted by the FOV/2 ghost separation. •After phased array processing the superimposed image and ghosts are separated to produce images weighted by their respective point spread values. At the top the ghost is nulled, while the bottom nulls the desired image. •The 2 PSF weighted images may be combined either coherently or non-coherently to form a final image. In the case of coherent combining the complex weights must be estimated.
•Before showing results, let me say a few words about comparing the effect of phase encode order on distortion. •This example is for short echo train multi-shot EPI using an echo train length of 4, which is typical in rapid cardiac imaging where echo train length are kept small due to T2* limitations. •These graphs illustrate the off-resonance phase versus ky for various phase encode orders, and their respective point spread functions. •For interleaved acquisition without echo-shifting, off-resonance gives rise to significant distortion. •This is mitigated with echo-shifting by smoothing the distortion at the price of extending the TR and there is still some geometric distortion remaining. •A non-interleaved or sequential order has rapid periodic phase errors which results in widely spaced ghosts. •Other distortion mechanisms listed which lead to blurring or smearing with the interleaved order, give rise to widely spaced ghosts with the non-interleaved order.
•This leads to a new strategy which I illustrated earlier when describing the block diagram used for phased array ghost cancellation. •By selecting a phase encode order such as non-interleaved, the k-space distortion due to a wide variety of mechanisms will be rapid and periodic leading to widely spaced ghosts. These ghosts can in turn be cancelled with the phased array method. •In addition to a reduction in TR, thus improved scan efficiency, there is also a reduction in distortion. •You can see by comparison that using the conventional interleaved echo shifted method, the oil region has a small chemical shift in the phase encode direction, that is eliminated by using the strategy of non-interleaved acquisition and ghost cancellation. •EPI flow artifacts using interleaved order are also translated to widely spaced ghosts which may then be cancelled.
•The phased array ghost cancellation method is predicated on knowing the complex coil sensitivity profiles which must be estimated. •Let me describe an adaptive method that we use to estimate the coil sensitivities which is applicable to cine or real time acquisition of multiple image frames. •A time varying phase encode order is used, for which the echo order is periodically varied in a cyclic fashion. •For instance, in the example shown with ETL=3, the order of echoes within each shot is 1,2,3 then 2,3,1 then 3,1,2 and so on. •After 3 frames, each ky line has been acquired at all echo delays. There are a number of ways to then reconstruct a lower temporal resolution, ghost suppressed image which can be used for calculating the phased array coefficients. In this illustration we simply use a low pass temporal filter. •The coefficients are then applied to the various images and their shifts to separate the ghosts, followed by combining.
•We applied the adaptive phased array ghost cancellation method to cardiac imaging using a gated segmented cine acquisition. •The pulse sequence used FISP to gain increased blood to myocardium contrast, and we used echo trains of both 2 and 3 echoes to increase the scan efficiency which may be traded off for a greater number of cardiac phases, reduced breath-hold time, or increased spatial resolution. •In the upper left the images with ghosts are shown, and along side are the separated images weighted by h0 and h1, the space variant point spread function. The ghosts are clearly greater in regions of fat. •The h0 and h1 weighted images may be combined as shown, or a small degree of fat suppression may be achieved by simply using the h0 weighted image by itself. •The interleaved echo time shifted image is shown for comparison. This method has increased TR with slightly reduced scan efficiency, greater FISP off-resonance banding artifacts, and greater flow artifacts, due largely to the larger phase encode blip size.
•The results are shown for an echo train length of 3.
•The raw images with ghosts prior to phased array cancellation, the individual separated ghosts, and combined magnitude are shown, as well as the conventional interleaved phase encode order with echo time shifting with increased TR for comparison. •The ETL=3 multi-echo FISP achieves a substantial gain in efficiency as compared to single echo. •The use of the phase array ghost cancellation method with multi-shot EPI provides a new strategy for acquisition, with reduced artifacts, and increased can efficiency.

•The ghost cancellation method does incur a penalty in SNR.
•The SNR loss or variance inflation is due to the ill-conditioning of the matrix inverse required to separate the ghosts, just as in the SENSE accelerated imaging method, except without an additional acceleration loss. The SNR loss depends on a number of factors including the number and spacing of ghosts, the number of coils and specific coil sensitivity profiles, the slice geometry, and noise statistics.
•The SNR loss is spatially varying.
•For the 4 coil system that we used, the SNR loss for ETL=2 (FOV/2 ghosts) was in the range of 10 to 40% depending on the image location.
•For ETL=3 the SNR loss with 4 coils was several fold higher.
•In the future, using 6 to 8 coils, we should be able to use ETL=3 or 4 with acceptable losses.
•In conclusion, the adaptive phased array ghost cancellation method permits new EPI design strategies which have benefits in reduced artifacts increased acquisition effiency. •Additionally, with greater design flexibility, you can use more optimal time ordering of shots, as for example in contrast enhanced imaging using saturation recovery •Finally, I would like to acknowledge Dan Herzka for his contribution to the implementation of multi-echo FISP imaging.
