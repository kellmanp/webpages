•First let me describe 2 problems which we are addressing.
•The first problem is that of setting the optimum inversion time (TI)
•The bottom figure corresponds to the ideal case, corresponding to phase sensitive detection without loss of polarity. The 3 curves represent the magnetization versus time for MI, blood, and normal at 15 minutes from administering contrast agent.
•The differences in T1 values are due to the different rates of washout of contrast agent which leads to the delayed hyper-enhancement
•The top figure illustrates the loss of polarity when using conventional, magnitude images.
•In order to optimize contrast between the MI and both blood and normal myocardium, it is best to set the inversion time (TI) to null the normal myocardium.
•Using conventional magnitude reconstruction, an error in the null time will cause a loss-of-contrast and polarity artifacts.
•Phase sensitive reconstruction can be used to maintain contrast and eliminate polarity artifacts over a wide range of null-time.
•Since the null time is a function of the patient and also the time from dose. Phase sensitive recon may eliminate several additional breath-holds to find a good setting. Furthermore, a nominal value may be used while imaging a stack of slices which takes several minutes, without loss-of-contast due to changing TI.
