•My talk will describe the benefits of phase sensitive reconstruction for detecting delayed hyper-enhancement of myocardial infarctions using inversion recovery with contrast agent •Despite the fact that, there are well known benefits of phase sensitive recontruction for inversion recovery imaging, it is not used commonly in practice •I will describe the methods we used to implement phase sensitive reconstruction and deal with the unique challenges of cardiac imaging
•First let me describe 2 problems which we are addressing.
•The first problem is that of setting the optimum inversion time (TI)
•The bottom figure corresponds to the ideal case, corresponding to phase sensitive detection without loss of polarity. The 3 curves represent the magnetization versus time for MI, blood, and normal at 15 minutes from administering contrast agent. •The differences in T1 values are due to the different rates of washout of contrast agent which leads to the delayed hyper-enhancement •The top figure illustrates the loss of polarity when using conventional, magnitude images. •In order to optimize contrast between the MI and both blood and normal myocardium, it is best to set the inversion time (TI) to null the normal myocardium. •Using conventional magnitude reconstruction, an error in the null time will cause a loss-of-contrast and polarity artifacts. •Phase sensitive reconstruction can be used to maintain contrast and eliminate polarity artifacts over a wide range of null-time. •Since the null time is a function of the patient and also the time from dose. Phase sensitive recon may eliminate several additional breath-holds to find a good setting. Furthermore, a nominal value may be used while imaging a stack of slices which takes several minutes, without loss-of-contast due to changing TI.
•The second problem we address is the variation in intensity due to the use of surface coils, which can be as much as 40% across the heart. •Ideally, the intensities for the MI, blood, and normal tissue would be the same regardless of position, as shown in the lower figure, such that one could adjust the window-level to null either normal or blood, and operate anywhere on this curve with fairly uniform contrast. •However, as illustrated by the top set of curves illustrating only +/-10% variation in intensity, the variation in blood for example is greater than MI-to-blood contrast. •The top set of images are not normalized for surface coil intensity variation. You see that as the level is increased to null the blood, the weaker region of MI at the inferior wall is lost. •In the lower set of images which are intensity normalized, the level can be adjusted to null either normal myocardium or blood while achieving a more uniform contrast.
•The next several slides show the methods we used.
•Phase sensitive, cardiac imaging poses a number of unique challenges to include:
•Rapid spatial variation in background phase due to …
•Motion due to heart and respiration
•In order to a measure the background phase for each pixel, we obtain a reference image at the same cardiac phase during the same breath-hold, as shown in this diagram
•We image using cardiac gated segmented acquisition with breathold over approx 12 heartbeats using an inversion pulse every 2 heart beats to allow for almost complete recovery, even for patients with elevated heart rates.
•In alternate heart beats we acquire a reference image after the magnetization has subtantially recovered. The reference uses a low flip angle (5 deg) so as not to rob too much magnetization. At this low flip angle the reference image is only slighty T1 weighted and is also used for surface coil intensity normalization.
•We acquire in late diastole to reduce effects of heart motion.
•Typical imaging parameters are listed.
•This slide descibes the phase sensitive reconstruction
•The magnitude images for the T1-weighted IR image and the reference are shown.
•The phase sensitive image is derived by rotating the phase of each pixel to remove the background phase, and then simply using the real image.
•Another benefit of this method is a slight improvement in SNR in regions of low SNR.
•We also use the multi-coil reference images to estimate complex B1-maps for optimum phase array combining.
•Both the IR and reference images are combined optimally
•before estimating the background phase, and
•before phase sensitive reconstruction.
•This slide descibes the normalization of surface coil intensity, which uses the magnitude of the reference image. •The phase sensitive image is normalized by dividing pixel-by-pixel using the reference magnitude. •A small amount of spatial smoothing is used to improve the SNR of the reference.
•This allows the level to be adjusted to set the null point.
•Using a 5 degree flip angle for the reference image, the reference variation due to T1 weighting is on the order of 10 to 15% variation, depending on the heart rate.
•This slide shows results comparing conventional magnitude images with normalized phase sensitive images. •These images are taken at approximately the same time from dose (nominally 15 minutes) while the TI is varied. •In the magnitude image with short TI you see the blood is nulled while the normal tissue is negative, but appears bright. •At the longer TI, the normal tissue is approx nulled and the blood appear bright. •For the central image the TI is somewhere in between and both normal tissue and blood appear bright. •The phase sensitive reconstructed images have polarity restored, and after adjusting the window and level, a consistently good contrast is achieved over a wide range of TI without artifacts due to incorrect polarity.
•This slide compares images taken at the same TI at different times from dose, over approximately 10 minutes. •In this example, the myocardium is approximately initially nulled at 13 minutes. •As the time from dose increases, the T1 increases and the contrast for a fixed TI is reduced. •The phase sensitive images achieve a uniformly good contrast without adjusting the inversion time. •A typical short axis stack with 8 slices may take as much as 5 minutes, over which time the optimum null time can vary enough to noticeably degrade the contrast. •Using phase sensitive reconstruction, it is possible to use a nominal value of TI, eliminate several breath-holds otherwise needed to find the optimal TI, and achieve a consistent good contrast.
•In conclusion, we have developed a phase sensitive method which we believe is both practical and has benefits •Using this method we can achieve uniformly good contrast by using nominal values of inversion time, and reduce the overalls number of scans. •We are also obtaining an improvement in SNR which you can see in these long axis images. •We have currently use this method on over 20 patients, and are beginning a quantitative assessment. •This has been recently implemented on 2 of our GE clinical scanners, a research scanner at NIH as well as a cardiac scanner at Suburban Hospital. The normalized phase sensitive, as well as the conventional magnitude images, are available immediately on-line to the cardiologists during the exam.
